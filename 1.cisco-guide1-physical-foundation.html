Cisco Fabric · Guide 1 · Section 01
Why leaf-spine? Not the old 3-tier design?
The mental model behind NovaCrestBank's fabric — understanding the design before touching a single cable
The key insight that changes how you think about networks
Traditional data center networking used a 3-tier design: access switches at the bottom, distribution switches in the middle, core switches at the top. This worked well when most traffic went north-south (server to internet). Modern data centers — especially those running VMware vMotion, vSAN, and container platforms like OpenShift — have mostly east-west traffic (server to server). Leaf-spine is designed specifically for east-west traffic dominance.

3-tier vs leaf-spine — side by side

Traditional 3-tier (old way)

Server A → access switch → distribution switch → core switch → distribution switch → access switch → Server B. That is 6 hops for server-to-server traffic. vMotion, vSAN replication, and pod-to-pod traffic all suffer from this latency.

Also: spanning tree. 3-tier networks use Spanning Tree Protocol (STP) to prevent loops — which means half your uplinks are blocked as standby. Wasted capacity.

Leaf-spine (NovaCrestBank's design)

Server A → leaf switch → spine switch → leaf switch → Server B. That is always exactly 2 hops, regardless of where the two servers are. Consistent, predictable, low latency.

Also: no spanning tree. The spine is pure Layer 3 (routing). Loops cannot form at Layer 3. All uplinks are active simultaneously via ECMP (Equal-Cost Multi-Path). Full bandwidth, all the time.

The two golden rules of leaf-spine

  • 1
    Every leaf connects to every spine — never to another leaf

    In NovaCrestBank's fabric: Leaf-1 connects to Spine-1 and Spine-2. Leaf-2 connects to Spine-1 and Spine-2. Leaf-3 connects to Spine-1 and Spine-2. Leaf-1 does NOT connect directly to Leaf-2 or Leaf-3. All leaf-to-leaf traffic goes via a spine. This guarantees the 2-hop maximum and allows the spines to do ECMP load balancing.

  • 2
    Spines are pure Layer 3 — they route, they do not switch

    The Nexus 9504 spine switches in NovaCrestBank run only IP routing. No VLANs, no MAC address tables, no spanning tree. Every interface on a spine switch is a routed (Layer 3) interface with its own IP address. VLANs are a leaf concern — only the leaf switches carry VLAN tags for the servers connected to them.

How NovaCrestBank traffic flows — concrete example

Real example — vSAN replication
ESXi Host 1 (on Leaf-1) writes a vSAN chunk. It must replicate to ESXi Host 3 (on Leaf-3). Path: Host 1 NIC → 25G link to Leaf-1 → Leaf-1 routes packet to Spine-1 (100G) → Spine-1 routes packet to Leaf-3 (100G) → Leaf-3 → Host 3 NIC. Two hops. Under 1ms latency. This is why vSAN works well on leaf-spine — the replication traffic gets consistent, low-latency paths. On a traditional 3-tier network, the same replication could take 6 hops with inconsistent latency.

Cable length is finite. A patch panel in the wrong rack means you need a 10-metre cable where a 1-metre one would do. Short cables mean less clutter, less signal degradation, easier troubleshooting. Rack placement principle — anti-affinity for critical pairs
Notice that Spine-01 and Spine-02 are both in Rack 2. This is acceptable because they are in the same physical cabinet with dual power feeds. However, in a stricter design, you would put one spine in Rack 1 and one in Rack 3 so that a rack-level power failure cannot take both spines. For NovaCrestBank's initial deployment, both spines in Rack 2 is the chosen design — the cabinet has dual PDUs on separate power circuits.

Why 6 NICs per ESXi host and why they are split this way
Each ESXi host has 6 × 25G NICs. They are paired into 3 purposes: vmnic0/vmnic1 handle ESXi management, vCenter management, and iSCSI/NFS. vmnic2/vmnic3 handle VM traffic and the vSAN/NSX-T overlay traffic (VXLAN). vmnic4/vmnic5 handle vSAN storage traffic exclusively — on a dedicated VLAN with dedicated bandwidth. The pairing (A and B) means both members of each pair go to the SAME leaf switch for the shortest path — they are bonded (LACP) for bandwidth and redundancy, not split across two leaf switches.

Cable types — DAC, fibre, and Cat6 explained
What each cable type is, when to use it, and exactly how to identify and handle it in the real world

The three cable types in NovaCrestBank

DAC — Direct Attach Copper (25G, short runs)

A DAC cable is a fixed cable with SFP28 transceivers permanently attached at both ends. You do not buy the cable and the transceivers separately — they come as one assembled unit. The electrical signal travels over the copper cable directly from one transceiver to the other without converting to optical (light).

When you use it: Any 25G connection where the two devices are in the same rack or within 5 metres of each other. In NovaCrestBank: leaf switch downlinks to ESXi hosts (same rack). DAC cables are cheaper than optical fibre + separate SFP transceivers and have less latency.

What it looks like: A thick, rigid black cable with a silver SFP28 module permanently crimped at each end. The cable itself is typically 1m, 2m, or 3m. You cannot change the transceivers — if the transceiver fails, replace the whole cable.

NovaCrestBank usage
Leaf-to-ESXi host connections (6 cables per host × 5 hosts = 30 DAC cables, all 25G, all 2m or 3m)

SMF — Single-Mode Fibre (100G, inter-rack)

Optical fibre carries data as pulses of laser light. Single-Mode Fibre (SMF) uses a very thin glass core (9 microns) that allows light to travel in a single straight path — suitable for long distances. In a data center, you use SMF for inter-rack connections because the signal can travel 100+ metres without degradation.

When you use it: Any 100G connection between racks — spine-to-leaf uplinks. The fibre cable itself is separate from the transceiver. You buy QSFP28 transceivers that plug into the switch port, and LC/LC fibre patch cables that plug into the transceivers.

What it looks like: A thin, yellow cable (SMF is always yellow; multimode is orange or aqua). LC connectors at each end — a small rectangular plug with a latch. Handle carefully: do not bend tightly, do not touch the glass end, always keep dust caps on unused connectors.

The transceiver: A QSFP28 (Quad Small Form-factor Pluggable 28 Gbps × 4 = 112 Gbps ≈ 100G) module. It has a gold-coloured body and pulls out of the switch port with a bail latch. Always use Cisco-approved optics — Nexus switches will alert on third-party optics but will still work if you use the override command.

NovaCrestBank usage
All spine-to-leaf uplinks (6 uplinks × 2 spines = 12 SMF cables, all 100G). Spine peer link (1 cable). Total: 13 SMF patch cables, all LC-LC, 10m (to reach between racks).

Cat6A — Copper Ethernet (1G, management only)

Standard Cat6A (Category 6A) copper Ethernet cable with RJ45 connectors. The same cable you plug into a laptop. Used for 1G management connections — iLO ports, switch mgmt0 interfaces, console server. Not used for production data traffic in NovaCrestBank — all production traffic runs on 25G or 100G.

What it looks like: The familiar blue, grey, or yellow Ethernet patch cable with clear plastic RJ45 plugs at each end. Always use pre-terminated (factory-made) patch cables in a data center — never field-terminate in a production environment. Pre-terminated cables are tested to spec. Field-terminated cables introduce variation and potential failure.

NovaCrestBank usage
All OOB management connections from Catalyst 9200 to iLO ports and mgmt0 interfaces. Console cables (RJ45 to RJ45 rollover cable) for initial device setup.

Fibre handling — the rules every data center engineer knows

  • Never bend fibre cable tighter than a 30mm radius. The glass core cracks under tight bends and the signal degrades permanently.
  • Always keep dust caps on unused fibre ports and on cables not in use. A single dust particle on a fibre end face causes signal loss. Clean fibre end faces with an IEC 61300-3-35 compliant cleaner before inserting.
  • Never pull a fibre cable by the cable itself — always push and pull by the connector body.
  • LC connectors have a small latch on top — press the latch while pulling to disconnect. Never yank without pressing the latch.
  • QSFP28 transceivers have a bail latch — rotate it 90 degrees downward to unlock before pulling the transceiver from the port.

Why VLANs must be planned before any switch is configured
A VLAN is a logical separation of traffic within the physical fabric. Getting VLAN IDs wrong after deployment means reconfiguring every switch, every ESXi host, every VM, and every IP address. Plan once, configure once. This plan becomes the source of truth for every team — networking, virtualisation, storage, and security — for the life of this data center.

The trunk configuration rule — which VLANs go on which port type

ESXi host downlink ports (Leaf → ESXi)

These are trunk ports carrying ALL production VLANs: 10, 20, 25, 30, 50, 60, 70. The ESXi host's virtual switch (vDS) then separates traffic onto different VMkernel adapters and VM port groups based on VLAN ID.

switchport mode trunk
switchport trunk allowed vlan 10,20,25,30,50,60,70

Spine uplink ports (Leaf → Spine)

These are routed (Layer 3) ports — no VLANs at all. Each uplink has its own /30 IP address. The spine runs BGP and routes between the leaf SVIs. No switchport command — instead no switchport.

no switchport
ip address 10.0.12.1/30
ip router ospf 1 area 0

Console access — your first connection to a new Nexus switch
Before any IP address is configured, you connect via console cable — exactly what you see and exactly what you do
Console access — the only way in on day zero
A brand-new Nexus switch has no IP address, no SSH keys, and no management configuration. The ONLY way to configure it for the first time is via the physical console port. You connect a laptop to the switch's console port with a rollover cable (RJ45 to RJ45), open a terminal emulator, and type commands directly into the switch's command line. This is called the setup wizard — you are about to see it in Guide 2.

What you need for console access

Console cable (rollover)

A blue RJ45-to-RJ45 cable (Cisco standard rollover cable) or a USB-to-RJ45 console adapter (more common on modern laptops). The Nexus switches have a console port labelled CON or CONSOLE on the front or rear panel. It looks like an Ethernet port but is NOT Ethernet — it is a serial console port.

Terminal emulator software

On Windows: PuTTY (free download). On macOS: screen command or Serial app. Connect at settings: 9600 baud, 8 data bits, no parity, 1 stop bit, no flow control (written as 9600 8N1). This is the standard console speed for Cisco Nexus switches.

USB-to-Serial adapter

Modern laptops have no RS-232 serial port. Use a USB-to-RS232 adapter plus a DB9-to-RJ45 converter, or use a purpose-built USB console cable (Cisco USB console cable, Part CONSADPT). Install the driver for the USB adapter — it appears as a COM port (Windows) or /dev/tty.usbserial (macOS).

Opening PuTTY for console access — step by step

  • 1
    Find your COM port number

    In Windows: Right-click Start → Device Manager → Ports (COM & LPT). Look for "USB Serial Port (COM3)" or similar. Note the COM number — you will need it in PuTTY. If you see nothing: the USB driver is not installed. Download the driver for your specific USB adapter.

  • 2
    Open PuTTY → Connection Type: Serial

    Open PuTTY. Under "Connection type" select Serial. In the "Serial line" box type COM3 (or your COM number). In the "Speed" box type 9600. Click "Open". A black terminal window opens.

  • 3
    Press Enter — the switch responds

    If the switch is already booted: press Enter once or twice. You will see the switch prompt or the setup wizard. If the switch is just powering on: wait 3–5 minutes for NX-OS to boot. You will see boot messages scrolling — do not press anything until the wizard appears.

What you will see when a Nexus switch boots for the first time
On first boot, NX-OS runs a setup wizard that asks you a series of questions. This is covered in detail in Guide 2. It takes about 10 minutes to answer all questions. After completing the wizard, the switch saves the config and you can then SSH to it via the management IP you set. From that point on, you use SSH instead of console — except for emergencies.

The rule — physical before logical
Configuration mistakes are recoverable. Physical mistakes (wrong cable in the wrong port, wrong SFP in the wrong slot) discovered after weeks of configuration are costly and sometimes require downtime to fix. Verify every physical connection before any software configuration begins.

Common causes: wrong cable type (DAC in an SFP28 slot that needs an SFP28, or vice versa), transceiver not fully seated (push it in further until you hear/feel it click), or speed mismatch (100G transceiver in a port configured for 10G).
