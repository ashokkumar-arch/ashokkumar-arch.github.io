Why vSAN and NFS VMkernel adapters have no default gateway
vmk0 has a gateway (10.10.0.1) and vmk1 (vMotion) has a gateway (10.20.0.1). Why do vmk2 and vmk3 have no gateway?
Because vSAN and NFS traffic never leaves the local subnet — all communication is host-to-host within the 10.25.0.0/24 or 10.30.0.0/24 subnet.

vSAN VMkernel adapters (vmk2 at 10.25.0.x) communicate only with other ESXi hosts' vmk2 adapters in the same /24 subnet. No vSAN traffic ever needs to be routed to another subnet — all vSAN replication, heartbeat, and metadata traffic is local. Setting a gateway would be harmless but meaningless, and could potentially cause ESXi to try routing vSAN traffic through the gateway (which would fail). Best practice: no gateway for storage VMkernels.

NFS VMkernel adapters (vmk3 at 10.30.0.x) connect to the NetApp AFF A400 NFS LIFs at 10.30.0.100 and 10.30.0.101 — both in the same /24 subnet. No routing needed. Same principle: no gateway.
VMware Series · vSphere-04 · Section 04
Execution order — why sequence matters in Phase 2
The order of operations is not arbitrary — it is designed to ensure that at every step, at least one path to each host remains operational
The Phase 2 execution sequence — and the reasoning
Phase 2 involves six distinct operations. Does the order matter?
Yes — significantly. The correct order keeps management connectivity operational at every step, and ensures each LACP bond is complete before the next is started.

Step 1: Complete LAG-Management (vmnic1 → Uplink2)
Management redundancy first. Currently only vmnic0 is in the management LAG. If vmnic0 fails during Phase 2 operations, management connectivity to that host is lost. Adding vmnic1 to the LAG immediately makes the management bond resilient — a failure of either NIC no longer causes a management outage.

Step 2: Create vMotion VMkernel (vmk1)
Before migrating vmnic2/3 to the vDS, the port group (PG-VLAN20-vMotion) they will carry traffic for must exist and have at least one test VMkernel on it. Creating vmk1 first confirms the VLAN 20 port group works before traffic-carrying NICs are moved to it.

Step 3: Migrate vmnic2/3 → LAG-VM-OCP (Uplink3/4)
VM production, vMotion, and OCP traffic now use dedicated NICs. vMotion becomes fully operational.

Step 4: Create vSAN and NFS VMkernels (vmk2, vmk3)
These VMkernels need to exist before the storage NICs are assigned — the port groups they use (VLAN 25, VLAN 30) must be confirmed working before vmnic4/5 are moved.

Step 5: Migrate vmnic4/5 → LAG-Storage (Uplink5/6)
Storage NICs move to the vDS. vSAN and NFS paths become active.

Step 6: Remove vSwitch0
Only after every VMkernel is on the vDS and every NIC is in a LAG. This is the final cleanup step — no traffic should be on vSwitch0 at this point.

VMware Series · vSphere-04 · Section 07
vMotion VMkernel — what it is and what it enables
Understanding the VMkernel adapter that makes live VM migration possible before creating it
What vMotion actually does at the network level
vMotion migrates a running VM between hosts. What does the VMkernel adapter actually carry during a migration?
During a vMotion migration, the contents of the source VM's RAM are iteratively copied across the network to the destination host while the VM continues running. The vMotion VMkernel adapter on each host carries this memory transfer.

The migration process: (1) vCenter instructs the source host (esxi01's vmk1 at 10.20.0.11) to begin copying the VM's memory pages to the destination host (esxi02's vmk1 at 10.20.0.12). (2) Pages that change while being copied are re-copied (dirty page tracking). (3) When the remaining dirty pages are small enough to copy in under a few milliseconds, the VM is briefly paused, the final pages are copied, and the VM resumes on the destination host. (4) The total pause time is typically under 1 second — invisible to users.

This transfer can involve gigabytes of data per migration. A 64 GB VM at 25 Gbps line rate takes approximately 20 seconds. That's why vMotion has dedicated NICs (vmnic2/3 in LAG-VM-OCP) and a dedicated VLAN — to prevent this bulk transfer from affecting management traffic or storage replication.

Why only "vMotion traffic" is ticked — not Fault Tolerance or Management
This VMkernel will be on the VM/OCP NIC pair. Could it also carry Fault Tolerance logging?
One VMkernel — one purpose. Each traffic type gets its own dedicated VMkernel adapter.

Fault Tolerance (FT) logging requires extremely low latency and extremely high bandwidth — an FT-protected VM sends every memory write to the secondary host in real time. FT logging and vMotion compete for the same NIC bandwidth. If both are on vmk1, a vMotion burst (gigabytes per migration) starves FT logging, causing FT secondary host synchronisation failures.

NovaCrestBank does not plan to use vSphere Fault Tolerance initially (it is a more restrictive HA mechanism than standard HA and requires single-vCPU VMs). If FT is needed in the future, a separate vmk adapter should be created for it. For now: vmk1 = vMotion only.

VMware Series · vSphere-04 · Section 12
vSAN VMkernel — what it carries and why MTU 9000 is mandatory
Understanding the storage network before creating it — the VMkernel adapter that vSAN uses for all distributed storage traffic
What traffic the vSAN VMkernel carries
What actually flows through the vSAN VMkernel adapter (vmk2)?
The vSAN VMkernel carries four types of traffic, all of which require stable, low-latency, high-throughput connectivity between hosts:

1. Data plane — read/write I/O. When a VM writes to its virtual disk, the data is sent from the host where the VM runs to the hosts that hold the vSAN disk stripes (typically 2 or 3 other hosts in the cluster). This is the highest-bandwidth component — sustained VM disk I/O generates continuous traffic on vmk2.

2. Resynchronisation traffic. When a host comes back online after maintenance or a failure, vSAN re-synchronises any data that changed while the host was offline. This can generate very high bandwidth for a sustained period — potentially saturating a 25G NIC pair for hours.

3. Heartbeat traffic. vSAN sends periodic heartbeat packets between hosts to detect host failures. These are small packets but extremely latency-sensitive — a missed heartbeat can trigger a fault domain recalculation.

4. Metadata and cluster state. Object placement decisions, witness traffic, and cluster health information.

All of this together is why vSAN gets dedicated NICs (vmnic4/5) and why MTU 9000 is required — large frames reduce per-packet overhead for bulk data transfers.

VMware Series · vSphere-04 · Section 14
Verify vSAN VMkernel — the MTU 9000 vmkping test
The single most important verification for storage networking — confirming jumbo frames traverse the entire path without fragmentation
This test must pass before vSAN is configured — a failed MTU test means silent data loss
If any hop in the path between two vSAN VMkernels has an MTU mismatch, vSAN traffic is silently dropped. vSAN will appear to function but with dramatically degraded performance, frequent resync storms, and potential data corruption. The vmkping -d -s 8972 test is non-negotiable — run it between every pair of hosts before enabling vSAN.
ESXi SSH — esxi01 — vSAN VMkernel MTU verification
# vmkping with -d (don't fragment) and -s 8972
# 8972 = 9000 (jumbo frame) - 28 bytes (IP + ICMP header overhead)

VMware Series · vSphere-04 · Section 15
NFS VMkernel — the path to the NetApp AFF A400
What the NFS VMkernel connects and why it needs the same MTU treatment as vSAN
Why NFS and vSAN are on the same NIC pair but separate VMkernels
vSAN and NFS both use vmnic4/5 through LAG-Storage. Why create separate VMkernels (vmk2 for vSAN, vmk3 for NFS) rather than one combined VMkernel?
Because each VMkernel service binding is what tells ESXi which traffic type a VMkernel carries — and each traffic type must be bound to exactly one VMkernel per host. You cannot tick both "vSAN traffic" and "NFS traffic" on the same VMkernel adapter and expect both to work correctly.

Additionally, separate VMkernels provide operational clarity: vmk2 is vSAN, vmk3 is NFS. When troubleshooting NFS connectivity, you ping from vmk3 to the NetApp LIF. When troubleshooting vSAN heartbeat issues, you inspect vmk2 statistics. If both were on one VMkernel, diagnosing per-service issues would require capturing all traffic on that VMkernel and filtering — unnecessarily complex.

Both VMkernels share the same physical NIC pair (vmnic4/5 through LAG-Storage) — the NIC pair serves both storage protocols simultaneously. This is correct: the LACP bond provides up to 50G aggregate bandwidth, and NFS and vSAN traffic are segregated at the software layer even though they share the physical path.

Configure NIOC traffic shares now that all VMkernels exist
NIOC was enabled in Phase 1 but could only be partially configured — now that vSAN and NFS VMkernels exist, the full traffic-type policy can be applied

Navigate to NCB-vDS-01 → Configure tab → Network I/O Control → Edit. Confirm or set the following shares policy which was partially configured in vSphere-02 but can now be verified against all active traffic types:

Traffic typeSharesReservationRationale
vSAN High (100) 0 Mbps Storage I/O has the highest latency sensitivity. High shares ensure vSAN is not starved by VM traffic bursts during peak load.
vMotion Normal (50) 0 Mbps vMotion benefits from high bandwidth but is not latency-critical. Shares ensure it completes in reasonable time without starving vSAN.
Management Normal (50) 100 Mbps The 100 Mbps hard reservation ensures management traffic is always served — critical during incidents when every other traffic type may be saturated.
Virtual Machine Normal (50) 0 Mbps VM traffic is best-effort. Gets what remains after storage and management are served. During normal operations (no contention) this is the majority of bandwidth.
NFS High (100) 0 Mbps NFS storage latency directly affects VM disk I/O on NetApp-hosted datastores. Treated identically to vSAN.
Why the management reservation is fixed Mbps but vSAN uses shares
NIOC supports both shares (proportional) and reservations (absolute Mbps). Why use a reservation for management but shares for everything else?
Because management traffic has a hard minimum requirement — it must work at any load level — while storage traffic needs proportional prioritisation relative to VM traffic, not absolute bandwidth guarantees.

Management traffic (SSH sessions, vCenter API calls, heartbeat packets) totals perhaps 10–50 Mbps under heavy use. A 100 Mbps reservation guarantees that even if a VM generates a 49G burst on the management NIC pair (consuming all but 1 Gbps of the 50G bond), the management VMkernel still gets its 100 Mbps. Without this reservation, it is possible for management to be starved to near-zero in extreme circumstances.

For vSAN and NFS, a fixed reservation would hold bandwidth unused during off-peak periods when no storage I/O is occurring. Shares are proportional — when vSAN traffic is heavy, it gets 2× the proportion of VM traffic (100 shares vs 50 shares). When vSAN traffic is light, all bandwidth goes to VMs. No waste.
