VCF Series · Section 01
Why VCF changes everything from classic vSphere
The mental shift required — from "VMs on a VLAN" to "software-defined everything on a physical fabric"
The fundamental difference — who owns the network
In classic vSphere, networking was: physical VLAN → vDS port group → VM NIC. Why does VCF change this model entirely?
In classic vSphere, the physical network engineer owned the network. VLANs were provisioned on Cisco switches, port groups were created on the vDS, and every new network segment required a change ticket to both teams. In VCF with NSX, the cloud/virtualisation team creates new logical networks in software — in seconds — without touching a single switch.

This matters for OpenShift specifically because:

OCP needs dozens of isolated network segments — one per namespace, one for pod-to-pod traffic, one for ingress, one for node management. In classic vSphere these would require dozens of VLANs on the physical switches, dozens of port groups on the vDS, and weeks of change management. In VCF + NSX, the OCP installer creates them all as NSX segments in minutes, automatically, with zero physical switch changes.

The physical network (Cisco fabric) becomes the "underlay" — it only carries two things: management traffic and NSX tunnel traffic. NSX builds all the actual VM and container networks as encrypted overlay tunnels on top of that physical fabric.
🔴 Application Layer — OpenShift
OCP API, ingress, pod networks, namespaces, banking applications — all live here as logical constructs. NSX creates and manages these automatically when OCP is installed with NSX-T integration.
🟣 NSX Overlay — Software-Defined Network
Tier-0 Gateway (BGP border), Tier-1 Gateways (tenant routing), Segments (logical switches), Load Balancer (API VIP + Ingress VIP), Distributed Firewall (microsegmentation). All created in the NSX Manager GUI — no physical switch changes required.
🔵 VCF Underlay — Physical VLANs
A small set of VLANs on the Cisco fabric for: VCF management traffic, NSX TEP (tunnel) traffic, and Edge uplink traffic. These are provisioned once during VCF bring-up and rarely change afterwards.
⚫ Physical Fabric — Cisco Leaf-Spine
The same Cisco Nexus leaf-spine fabric from the previous series. In VCF mode it carries fewer VLANs (just underlay VLANs) but at higher bandwidth — all NSX overlay traffic encapsulated as VXLAN/GENEVE tunnels on top of the physical links.
Key mental shift — VLANs are for infrastructure, not for applications
In the previous vSphere series, every application had its own VLAN: VLAN 50 for OCP nodes, VLAN 60 for production VMs, VLAN 70 for Windows servers. In VCF + NSX, physical VLANs are only for infrastructure (VCF management, NSX tunnels, Edge uplinks). Applications live on NSX logical segments — which look and behave like VLANs to the VMs and pods, but are software-defined and require no physical VLAN provisioning.
VCF Series · Section 02
The two networks in VCF — underlay and overlay
Understanding these two concepts is the single most important mental model for everything that follows — every design decision flows from this distinction

Underlay Network

What it is: The physical network. Cisco leaf-spine switches, physical cables, physical VLANs. This is the infrastructure that existed before VCF was installed.

What it carries in VCF: Only three types of traffic:
1. VCF management (SDDC Manager, vCenter, NSX Manager communicating with each other)
2. NSX TEP traffic — the GENEVE-encapsulated overlay tunnel packets
3. Edge uplink traffic — external routing between NSX and the physical world

Who configures it: The network team. Done once during VCF bring-up. Rarely changes.

Overlay Network (NSX)

What it is: The software-defined network that NSX builds on top of the underlay. Logical segments, gateways, and firewalls that live entirely in software — no physical VLANs needed.

What it carries: All VM and container traffic. OCP node-to-node communication, pod networks, external application traffic — all encapsulated in GENEVE tunnels running over the physical fabric.

Who configures it: The virtualisation/cloud team — in the NSX Manager GUI. A new network segment can be created in 60 seconds without touching the physical network.

What is GENEVE and why does NSX use it instead of VXLAN
The previous OCP guide used physical VLANs. NSX uses GENEVE tunnelling. What is GENEVE and what does the candidate need to understand about it operationally?
GENEVE (Generic Network Virtualisation Encapsulation) is the tunnel protocol NSX uses to carry overlay traffic across the physical network. A VM's Ethernet frame is wrapped inside a GENEVE header and a UDP packet, sent across the physical network to the destination ESXi host's TEP (Tunnel Endpoint), unwrapped, and delivered to the destination VM — exactly as if the two VMs were on the same physical network segment, even though they may be on different ESXi hosts with no physical VLAN between them.

What the candidate needs to understand operationally:

1. Every ESXi host that participates in NSX has a TEP (Tunnel Endpoint) — a special VMkernel interface that sends and receives GENEVE packets. This is configured on a dedicated VLAN (the TEP VLAN — covered in Section 10).

2. TEP traffic requires MTU 1600 minimum on the physical network (to accommodate the GENEVE encapsulation overhead on top of the inner 1500-byte Ethernet frame). The Cisco fabric must have its MTU configured for at least 1600, ideally 9000 (jumbo frames) end-to-end.

3. The candidate never sees GENEVE packets directly — NSX hides all encapsulation/decapsulation inside the ESXi kernel. From the VM's perspective, it sends and receives normal Ethernet frames.
VCF Series · Section 03
Workload Domains — correcting and completing the mental model
The candidate's three-domain assumption was close — this section aligns it precisely with VCF reality
The candidate's intuition was correct on the important point
The candidate correctly identified that there is a management domain and that OpenShift gets its own domain with 5 ESXi hosts. The main correction is that Windows infrastructure VMs (DCs, CAs) live inside the Management Domain as regular VMs — they do not need their own Workload Domain.

NovaCrestBank's VCF topology — two domains

Management Domain

Created automatically during VCF bring-up. Cannot be deleted. Runs VCF's own management plane.

What runs here:

  • SDDC Manager (VCF's orchestrator)
  • Management vCenter (vcenter-mgmt.novacrestbank.local)
  • NSX Manager cluster (3 NSX Manager VMs)
  • NSX Edge cluster (for Tier-0/Tier-1 routing)
  • DC01 and DC02 (Active Directory)
  • Root CA and Issuing CA
  • All other infrastructure service VMs

ESXi hosts required: 4 minimum (VMware validated minimum for production Management Domain)

OpenShift VI Workload Domain

Created after Management Domain is healthy. A separate vCenter instance, separate NSX configuration (or shared NSX — NovaCrestBank uses shared NSX from the Management Domain).

What runs here:

  • Workload vCenter (vcenter-ocp.novacrestbank.local)
  • OpenShift master nodes (3)
  • OpenShift infrastructure nodes (3)
  • OpenShift worker nodes (3+)
  • Future banking application VMs

ESXi hosts: 5 (as planned)

Shared NSX vs per-domain NSX — why NovaCrestBank uses shared
VCF allows each Workload Domain to have its own NSX instance or share the Management Domain's NSX. Which does NovaCrestBank use?
Shared NSX — the Management Domain's NSX instance also manages the OpenShift Workload Domain's networking. This is the correct choice for NovaCrestBank's scale and reduces operational complexity.

Per-domain NSX: each Workload Domain has its own 3-node NSX Manager cluster. This means two sets of NSX Manager VMs to maintain, two sets of certificates to rotate, two control planes to monitor. Appropriate for very large enterprises or where strict operational separation is required (e.g., different teams manage each domain).

Shared NSX (NovaCrestBank's choice): the same 3-node NSX Manager cluster that the Management Domain uses also manages the OpenShift Workload Domain. The NSX Manager GUI has one pane of glass for all networking — Management Domain VMs and OpenShift nodes are visible together. At NovaCrestBank's scale (9 ESXi hosts total), one NSX instance is the correct and simpler architecture.
PCI-DSS v4.0 Requirement 1.3.2 — Network segmentation between management and cardholder data environments
Having the OpenShift Workload Domain as a separate VCF domain — with its own vCenter and distinct NSX segment namespace — provides the administrative and logical separation between the management plane (SDDC Manager, vCenter, NSX Manager) and the cardholder data environment (OCP running banking applications). NSX Distributed Firewall then enforces the technical controls within and between these environments.

Why two Edge Nodes — not one
An Edge Node runs the Tier-0 Gateway. Why are two required?
For Active-Active or Active-Standby redundancy of the Tier-0 Gateway. If Edge Node 1 fails, Edge Node 2 takes over BGP peering with the Cisco fabric within seconds — the NSX overlay continues routing traffic with no interruption.

A single Edge Node is a single point of failure for all external routing. Any VM or OCP pod trying to reach the internet, DNS servers, or any IP outside the NSX overlay depends on the Edge Node's BGP adjacency to the Cisco fabric. One Edge Node failure = complete loss of external connectivity for all NSX-hosted workloads. Two Edge Nodes = HA for external routing.

NIST SP 800-53 SC-5 Denial of Service Protection — redundant routing components prevent a single hardware failure from causing a denial of service across banking workloads.

The Cisco leaf-spine fabric from the previous series continues to be used — but its job description changes significantly

Critical insight — Cisco fabric carries far fewer VLANs in VCF mode
In the previous classic vSphere series, the Cisco fabric needed VLANs for: management (10), vMotion (20), vSAN (25), NFS (30), OOB (40), OCP nodes (50), production VMs (60), Windows servers (70). That is 8 VLANs.

In VCF mode, the Cisco fabric needs VLANs for: management (10), vMotion (20), vSAN (25), TEP — Host (100), TEP — Edge (101), Edge Uplink A (102), Edge Uplink B (103), OOB (40). That is 8 VLANs too — but notice that VLANs 50, 60, and 70 (the application VLANs) are gone. All application networks are now NSX overlay segments. The physical fabric gets simpler; the software layer absorbs the complexity.

VCF Series · Section 06
Underlay — what it is and why each VLAN exists
Understanding the purpose of each underlay VLAN before seeing the complete plan — so the plan makes sense rather than being a list to memorise

The underlay VLANs — each one explained

VLAN 10 — ESXi Management

Every ESXi host needs a management VMkernel (vmk0) on this VLAN. SDDC Manager communicates with ESXi hosts on this VLAN. vCenter uses this VLAN to manage ESXi. NSX Manager deploys the NSX VIB to ESXi hosts via this VLAN. This is the same VLAN as in the previous series — no change.

VLAN 20 — vMotion

Live migration of VMs between ESXi hosts. Same as before. Required for DRS and maintenance mode operations. Same as previous series.

VLAN 25 — vSAN

vSAN inter-host communication for the storage cluster. Both the Management Domain and the OpenShift Workload Domain have their own independent vSAN clusters. The Management Domain's vSAN uses esxi-mgmt-01 through 04; the OCP Domain's vSAN uses esxi-ocp-01 through 05. Both clusters use the same VLAN 25 — they are on separate IP subnets within that VLAN. Requires MTU 9000 (jumbo frames) end-to-end on the Cisco fabric.

VLAN 100 — Host TEP (Tunnel Endpoint) — NEW in VCF

This is the most important new VLAN compared to the previous series. Every ESXi host that participates in NSX gets a TEP VMkernel interface on this VLAN. The TEP is where the ESXi host sends and receives GENEVE-encapsulated overlay packets.

When VM-A on esxi-ocp-01 sends a packet to VM-B on esxi-ocp-03, the packet is: encapsulated in GENEVE by esxi-ocp-01's TEP → sent across the physical fabric on VLAN 100 → received by esxi-ocp-03's TEP → decapsulated → delivered to VM-B. The physical fabric sees only a UDP packet on VLAN 100 — it has no visibility into the inner VM traffic.

Requires MTU 1600 minimum, ideally 9000 (jumbo frames).

VLAN 101 — Edge TEP — NEW in VCF

The Edge Nodes (edge-01 and edge-02) have their own TEP interfaces — separate from the host TEPs — on this VLAN. Edge TEPs connect the Edge Nodes to the NSX overlay fabric. Traffic flowing from the NSX overlay to the external network (and vice versa) passes through the Edge TEP.

VLANs 102 and 103 — Edge Uplink-A and Edge Uplink-B — NEW in VCF

The Edge Nodes have physical uplink interfaces that connect them to the Cisco leaf switches for BGP peering. Two uplinks (A and B) for redundancy — each on its own VLAN. The Edge Nodes peer with the Cisco leaf switches on these VLANs, advertising NSX segment routes into the physical fabric via BGP.

These are the "exit points" from the NSX overlay to the physical world. Traffic from an OCP pod destined for the internet travels: pod → NSX segment → Tier-1 Gateway → Tier-0 Gateway → Edge Uplink VLAN → Cisco leaf switch → Palo Alto NGFW → internet.

NSX Load Balancer replaces keepalived — why this matters
In the previous OCP installation, the API VIP and Ingress VIP were managed by keepalived running inside the OCP nodes. In VCF + NSX, the NSX Load Balancer replaces keepalived. What changes?
keepalived is a Linux process running inside OCP master nodes that broadcasts a VRRP announcement to claim a VIP. It is a simple mechanism but has limitations: it requires the VIP to be on the same subnet as the nodes, it has slower failover than hardware load balancers, and it has no SSL offloading or health check visibility. The NSX Load Balancer is a dedicated component in the Edge cluster — outside OCP — that provides enterprise-grade load balancing.

What changes operationally:
1. The VIPs (10.50.0.200 and 10.50.0.201) are now NSX Load Balancer Virtual Servers — configured in NSX Manager, not in OCP.
2. Health checks: NSX LB actively probes the master nodes and infra nodes. If a master fails, NSX LB detects it within seconds and stops sending traffic to that node — even before OCP's own HA mechanisms respond.
3. install-config.yaml still references the same VIP addresses — OCP does not need to know whether the VIP is served by keepalived or NSX LB. The IP addresses are identical.

PCI-DSS v4.0 Req 6.4 — Load balancers with active health checking reduce the risk of session establishment to failed or degraded components, protecting cardholder data from routing to unavailable services.
VCF Series · Section 10
TEP addressing — the NSX tunnel endpoint pools
The TEP is the new concept compared to classic vSphere — every host and every Edge Node gets one, and they need IP addresses from a dedicated pool
What a TEP is and why it needs its own IP pool
In classic vSphere, a VMkernel had one job (vSAN, vMotion, or management). What is a TEP VMkernel and why does it need its own VLAN and IP pool?
A TEP (Tunnel Endpoint) is a VMkernel interface on each ESXi host that NSX uses exclusively for GENEVE overlay traffic. Every ESXi host participating in NSX gets a TEP VMkernel automatically when NSX prepares the host. TEPs communicate with each other — every ESXi host's TEP must be able to reach every other ESXi host's TEP over the physical network.

Why a dedicated VLAN and IP pool:

1. Isolation: TEP traffic is high-volume and latency-sensitive — it carries all VM traffic between hosts. Isolating it on a dedicated VLAN prevents it from competing with management traffic for bandwidth. NIST SP 800-53 SC-8 — Transmission Confidentiality and Integrity: GENEVE encapsulation with optional encryption on the TEP VLAN provides in-transit protection for all overlay traffic.

2. IP pool: NSX requires a static IP pool for TEPs — a range of addresses from which TEP IPs are automatically assigned when NSX prepares each ESXi host. You define the pool in NSX Manager; NSX assigns IPs to hosts automatically.

3. No gateway needed: TEPs communicate host-to-host within the same VLAN — they do not need routing (no gateway IP). This is why VLAN 100 has no gateway in the VLAN plan. All TEPs are on the same /24 subnet and communicate directly at Layer 2 within the data centre.

NSX overlay — what NSX builds on top of the underlay
Introducing the NSX logical components — the building blocks that will be created in NSX-01 through NSX-05

The NSX logical components — from bottom to top

  • T0
    Tier-0 Gateway — the BGP border router

    The Tier-0 Gateway (T0) runs on the Edge Nodes and peers with the Cisco leaf switches via BGP. It is the "exit point" from the NSX overlay to the physical world. External traffic (internet, data centre core, Palo Alto NGFW) all passes through the T0. NovaCrestBank has one T0: NCB-T0-Gateway. It peers with Leaf-01 and Leaf-02 on the Edge Uplink VLANs (102 and 103).

  • T1
    Tier-1 Gateways — tenant routers

    A Tier-1 Gateway (T1) connects to the T0 and provides routing for a group of related segments. NovaCrestBank has two T1 gateways: NCB-T1-Infra (for management/infrastructure segments) and NCB-T1-OCP (for all OpenShift segments). The T1 does not need to peer with BGP — it advertises its connected segments to the T0 automatically via the NSX control plane.

  • SEG
    Segments — logical switches (equivalent to VLANs)

    Segments are the logical networks that VMs and OCP nodes connect to. They behave exactly like VLANs — a VM on a segment gets an IP, can communicate with other VMs on the same segment, and reaches other segments through the T1 gateway. NovaCrestBank has: seg-infra-services (connected to NCB-T1-Infra) and seg-ocp-nodes (connected to NCB-T1-OCP).

  • LB
    NSX Load Balancer — virtual servers on the Edge cluster

    The NSX Load Balancer runs on the Edge cluster and provides Layer 4 (TCP) and Layer 7 (HTTP/HTTPS) load balancing. NovaCrestBank configures two Virtual Servers: one for the OCP API VIP (10.50.0.200:6443) and one for the OCP Ingress VIP (10.50.0.201:443 and :80).

  • DFW
    Distributed Firewall — microsegmentation in the hypervisor kernel

    The NSX Distributed Firewall (DFW) runs inside every ESXi host's kernel — not in a network appliance. Every packet from every VM passes through the DFW before it enters the network. This means firewall rules can be applied at the individual VM level (or OCP namespace level) — not just at the network perimeter. PCI-DSS v4.0 Req 1.3 — network access controls between trusted and untrusted zones; DFW enforces these within the cluster without any physical firewall appliance.

    Why Active-Active HA mode for the T0 — not Active-Standby
    NSX Tier-0 supports Active-Active and Active-Standby HA modes. Why does NovaCrestBank use Active-Active?
    Active-Active uses both Edge Nodes simultaneously for traffic forwarding — doubling the available bandwidth and providing faster failover. Active-Standby keeps one Edge Node idle until the active one fails — simpler, but wastes half the capacity.

    For NovaCrestBank's Edge Nodes (Large size, 8 vCPU each): Active-Active means OCP ingress traffic distributes across both Edge Nodes via ECMP (Equal-Cost Multi-Path) — the same technique the Cisco spine-leaf fabric uses for uplink load balancing. If edge-01 fails, all traffic continues through edge-02 without any VIP migration or failover timeout.

    Active-Standby would be simpler (no ECMP to manage) but would leave one Edge Node idle. For a banking production environment with significant OCP ingress traffic, the additional capacity of Active-Active is worth the marginal configuration complexity.

    NIST SP 800-53 SC-5 and CP-7 — Denial of Service Protection and Alternate Processing Site: redundant and active load-sharing components ensure that no single Edge Node failure can interrupt service.

    Tier-1 Gateways and Segments — the tenant networks
    Two T1 gateways serve two distinct security domains — infrastructure services and OpenShift

    NCB-T1-Infra

    Purpose: Routes for infrastructure service VMs — DCs, CAs, DNS, DHCP servers.

    Connected segment: seg-infra-services (10.70.0.0/24)

    HA mode: Active-Standby (one T1 active at a time — simpler for stateful services like DNS)

    Route advertisement to T0: Connected segments (10.70.0.0/24) automatically advertised

    NCB-T1-OCP

    Purpose: Routes for all OpenShift workloads — OCP nodes, pod CIDR, service CIDR.

    Connected segments: seg-ocp-nodes (10.50.0.0/24)

    HA mode: Active-Standby (OCP nodes maintain their own session state — T1 failover is transparent)

    Route advertisement to T0: 10.50.0.0/24, and the OCP pod/service CIDRs (10.128.0.0/14, 172.30.0.0/16)

    Why two T1 gateways — why not connect everything to one T1
    Both NCB-T1-Infra and NCB-T1-OCP connect to the same NCB-T0-Gateway. Why have two T1s instead of one?
    Because the two T1 gateways represent two separate security domains with different trust levels and different firewall policy sets. Separating them at the T1 level means NSX Distributed Firewall can enforce different policies for each domain — and traffic between the two domains (e.g., OCP pods querying DC01 for LDAP authentication) passes through the T0, making it visible and controllable.

    With one T1: infrastructure VMs and OCP nodes are on the same routing domain. Traffic between them never leaves the T1 — it is routed internally. DFW can still control it, but there is no natural boundary that reflects the different trust levels.

    With two T1s: traffic from OCP nodes to DC01 must traverse T0 → T1-Infra → seg-infra-services. This inter-T1 path creates a natural policy enforcement point. In the NSX DFW guide (NSX-05), gateway firewall rules on NCB-T1-OCP will explicitly allow only specific OCP → infrastructure traffic (LDAPS on 636, DNS on 53) and deny everything else.

    PCI-DSS v4.0 Req 1.3.2 — Restrict inbound and outbound traffic to that which is necessary for the cardholder data environment. The two-T1 design enforces this at the network routing level, not just at the firewall rule level.
