HA vs DR vs Business Continuity — three distinct disciplines, three different teams
High Availability, Disaster Recovery, and Business Continuity are frequently conflated — even by experienced engineers. They address different failure scenarios, are owned by different teams, operate on different timescales, and require different technical solutions. Conflating them leads to designing systems that excel at one dimension while leaving the organisation exposed on the others.

The most dangerous confusion — treating HA as a substitute for DR
A common executive-level misunderstanding: "We have 3 replicas and Ceph 3× replication, so we don't need DR." HA prevents single-component failures from causing outages. It does not protect against: accidental data deletion (all 3 replicas delete the corrupted data simultaneously), ransomware (all 3 replicas encrypt the data simultaneously), software bugs that corrupt data across all replicas (GitLab misconfiguration that corrupts Gitaly — affects all replicas at once), or physical disasters that destroy all nodes. Backups (OADP) and restore procedures (this guide) are the answer to these scenarios. HA and DR are complementary, not substitutes for each other.
STORAGE-04 · Section 02
RTO and RPO — definition, ownership, and how values are determined
Recovery Time Objective (RTO) and Recovery Point Objective (RPO) are the two fundamental parameters of any DR strategy. They are not technical parameters — they are business decisions expressed in a form that allows technology teams to design appropriate solutions. Understanding who sets them and why prevents the common failure mode of engineers setting targets based on what is technically convenient rather than what the business requires.

RTO — Recovery Time Objective

Definition: The maximum acceptable duration of service unavailability after a disaster event before the organisation suffers unacceptable harm. Measured in hours or minutes.

What it governs technically: The time from "disaster declared" to "system serving production traffic." This drives: backup frequency (more frequent backups enable faster restore from a more recent state), infrastructure pre-provisioning (a recovery environment that must be built from scratch takes longer than one that is pre-provisioned and warm), and runbook quality (a tested, rehearsed restore procedure is faster than one run for the first time).

Example: RTO of 4 hours for payment processing means: if a disaster occurs at 09:00, payment processing must be restored and serving transactions by 13:00.

RPO — Recovery Point Objective

Definition: The maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time. The RPO defines how old the most recent recoverable backup can be. An RPO of 1 hour means the organisation accepts losing at most 1 hour of data in a disaster.

What it governs technically: Backup frequency — if the RPO is 1 hour, backups must run at least hourly. Storage architecture — if the RPO is 15 minutes, synchronous replication to a secondary site may be required (backup-based recovery cannot achieve sub-15-minute RPO at scale).

Example: RPO of 1 hour for payment processing means: if a disaster destroys the payment database at 10:45, the restored database may be missing up to 1 hour of transactions (from the 09:45 backup to 10:45). Those transactions must either be reconstructed from alternative records or accepted as lost.

Who sets RTO and RPO — and why engineers do not

Step 1
Business Impact Analysis (BIA)
The Business Continuity Management team conducts a BIA for every critical system. The BIA asks: what is the financial cost per hour of this system being down? What are the regulatory consequences? What is the reputational impact? What is the minimum set of functions that must continue? For NovaCrestBank's payment processing system: every hour of downtime represents lost transaction revenue, potential RBA notification obligations, potential customer compensation, and reputational risk. The BIA converts these impacts into a criticality tier and a target RTO/RPO.
Step 2
Regulatory baseline — APRA CPS 234
APRA (Australian Prudential Regulation Authority) CPS 234 mandates that regulated financial institutions maintain documented recovery objectives for information assets classified as critical or important. APRA does not prescribe specific RTO/RPO values — instead it requires that the bank's targets are commensurate with the criticality of the system and that the bank can demonstrate (through testing) that it can meet those targets. The RBA (Reserve Bank of Australia) separately mandates that systemically important payment providers maintain availability commitments. These regulatory requirements set a floor below which NovaCrestBank cannot set its targets.
Step 3
Technical feasibility assessment — technology's input
The platform engineering team receives the business-desired RTO/RPO targets and performs a feasibility assessment: can these targets be met with the current architecture? What would it cost to meet them? An RTO of 15 minutes for the entire banking platform would require a hot standby cluster with synchronous data replication — a significant infrastructure investment. An RTO of 4 hours is achievable with OADP backup-based restore from ODF MCG object storage. The technology team presents options with cost estimates, and the business decides which option to fund. The final targets are a negotiated agreement between business requirements, regulatory obligations, and technical capability.
Step 4
Annual testing and attestation
APRA CPS 234 requires that DR plans are tested at least annually. For NovaCrestBank, this means: once per year, the platform engineering team performs a DR exercise — restoring from OADP backup in a test environment and measuring the actual RTO achieved. The result is documented and reported to the Board Risk Committee and to APRA as part of the annual prudential reporting. If the actual RTO exceeds the target, this is a material risk finding that must be remediated. Section 17 of this guide covers the DR exercise procedure.
STORAGE-04 · Section 03
NovaCrestBank's RTO/RPO targets — BIA tiers and APRA obligations
NovaCrestBank classifies its systems into three tiers based on the Business Impact Analysis. Each tier has defined RTO and RPO targets that drive the backup frequency and recovery architecture in this guide.
Tier 1 — Critical

Regulatory obligation to maintain. Outage has immediate financial and regulatory consequence.

RTO: 4 hours
RPO: 1 hour

Systems: Payment processing (payment-service + banking PostgreSQL), Core banking database, NPP payment rail integration

Tier 2 — Important

Significant operational impact. Extended outage affects customer service and internal operations.

RTO: 24 hours
RPO: 4 hours

Systems: GitLab (source code), OCP platform itself, fraud detection, account management, observability stack

Tier 3 — Standard

Operational impact but not customer-facing or regulatory. Can be rebuilt from source.

RTO: 72 hours
RPO: 24 hours

Systems: Jenkins (pipelines), monitoring dashboards, GitLab CI artifacts, developer tooling

How the tiers drive backup architecture

Tier 1 — RPO 1 hourHourly OADP Schedule for banking PostgreSQL PVC snapshots. Etcd backup every 6 hours. Between snapshots, the RPO is managed by PostgreSQL WAL (Write-Ahead Log) archiving to ODF MCG — providing near-continuous recovery point.
Tier 2 — RPO 4 hours6-hourly OADP Schedule for GitLab (Gitaly + PostgreSQL). Etcd backup included in the 6-hourly cycle. The OCP cluster itself is backed up by the etcd snapshot, which allows full cluster state restoration.
Tier 3 — RPO 24 hoursDaily OADP Schedule for Jenkins, monitoring, and developer tooling. GitLab CI artifacts are not backed up by OADP — they can be regenerated by re-running pipelines.
APRA attestation requirementAnnual DR exercise must demonstrate actual recovery within the declared RTO. Results documented and reported to Board Risk Committee. OADP restore timing is measured and recorded.
STORAGE-04 · Section 04
etcd backup — beyond the kubectl command — the enterprise requirements
UPG-01 Section 18 introduced etcd backup as an operational procedure: run cluster-backup.sh on a master node before an upgrade. That is the correct Kubernetes-native approach for pre-upgrade safety. The enterprise DR model requires something fundamentally different: scheduled, automated, monitored, off-node, retention-managed, tested backups that satisfy regulatory obligations and are operational without human initiation.
The gap between the UPG-01 etcd backup and the enterprise DR requirement
UPG-01 Section 18 showed how to take an etcd snapshot with cluster-backup.sh. That backup is stored in /home/core/assets/backup/ on the master node that ran the script. What are the problems with this approach as an enterprise DR solution?
Five specific gaps between the UPG-01 procedure and enterprise DR requirements:

1. Manual execution. The UPG-01 backup runs when a platform engineer remembers to run it — typically only before upgrades. An enterprise RPO of 4 hours requires the backup to run automatically every 4 hours (or more frequently), continuously, with no human initiation. A backup that depends on human memory is not a backup — it is a wish.

2. On-node storage. The backup snapshot is stored at /home/core/assets/backup/ on the master node that ran the script. If that master node is destroyed by the same disaster that takes down the cluster (power failure, fire, ransomware), the backup is destroyed too. A backup stored on the system it protects is not a backup — it is a second copy of the same failure domain. Enterprise DR requires the backup to be stored off-node, in a durable, independent location (ODF MCG object storage in this case).

3. No monitoring or alerting. If the backup fails silently, no one knows. An organisation that believes it has hourly etcd backups but whose CronJob has been silently failing for 3 weeks discovers this at the worst possible moment — during a real DR event. Enterprise backup systems are monitored: a missed or failed backup fires an alert that is actioned within a defined SLA.

4. No retention management. Without automatic cleanup, old backup files accumulate and eventually fill the storage. Alternatively, if backups are manually deleted, the organisation loses recovery points. Enterprise backup has defined retention: keep the last N backups, or keep backups for M days.

5. Untested restore procedure. A backup whose restore procedure has never been executed in a simulated disaster scenario is an unknown. The enterprise requirement is that the restore procedure is documented as a runbook, tested annually in a DR exercise, and updated when the cluster changes. Section 06 provides the complete etcd restore runbook.

What the enterprise etcd backup system provides

Backup scheduleCronJob runs every 6 hours: 00:00, 06:00, 12:00, 18:00 UTC. Satisfies Tier 2 RPO of 4 hours with buffer.
Backup storageODF MCG ObjectBucketClaim — ncb-etcd-backups bucket. Off-node, Ceph 3× replication, independent of the OCP cluster's worker nodes. The backup survives the failure of all master nodes.
Backup contentetcd snapshot file + kubeconfig + static-pod-resources.tar.gz (the complete set produced by cluster-backup.sh). This triple is everything needed to restore the cluster to the snapshot's state.
Retention policy14 days. 4 backups/day × 14 days = 56 backup sets retained. Lifecycle rule on the MCG bucket automatically expires objects after 15 days.
Backup verificationAfter each backup, the CronJob reads back the uploaded object and validates the SHA-256 checksum. A checksum mismatch fires a PrometheusRule alert.
MonitoringTwo PrometheusRules: EtcdBackupMissed (fires if no successful backup in 7 hours — exceeding the RPO window) and EtcdBackupFailed (fires immediately on CronJob failure).
Restore procedureDocumented runbook (Section 06) tested annually. Estimated restore time: 45–90 minutes for a 3-master cluster.
STORAGE-04 · Section 05
Automated etcd backup CronJob — schedule, upload to ODF MCG, and alert
The etcd backup CronJob runs on master nodes (using a DaemonSet-style privileged pod that can execute on the control plane), runs cluster-backup.sh, uploads the output to ODF MCG, verifies the upload, and reports its result as a Prometheus metric. The entire flow is automated.
etcd backup CronJob — every 6 hours, uploads to ODF MCG
apiVersion: batch/v1
kind: CronJob
metadata:
  name: etcd-backup
  namespace: openshift-etcd
spec:
  schedule: "0 */6 * * *"
    # Every 6 hours at minute 0: 00:00, 06:00, 12:00, 18:00 UTC
    # Choose UTC midnight/6/12/18 — predictable, avoids peak banking hours (AEST morning).
  concurrencyPolicy: Forbid
    # Forbid: if the previous backup job is still running when the next one starts,
    # skip the new one. Prevents overlapping backup jobs from corrupting each other's output.
  successfulJobsHistoryLimit: 5
  failedJobsHistoryLimit: 3
  jobTemplate:
    spec:
      template:
        spec:
          serviceAccountName: etcd-backup
          hostNetwork: true
          hostPID: true
                    # hostNetwork and hostPID: required to execute cluster-backup.sh.
                      # cluster-backup.sh calls etcdctl via the static etcd pod,
                      # which requires host network and process namespace access.
                      # This pod runs on a master node only (nodeSelector below).
          nodeSelector:
            node-role.kubernetes.io/master: ""
          tolerations:
            - effect: NoSchedule
              key: node-role.kubernetes.io/master
          restartPolicy: OnFailure
          containers:
            - name: etcd-backup
              image: harbor.apps.ocp.corp.novacrestbank.com/ubi9/ubi:latest
              securityContext:
                privileged: true
                                # privileged: true required to run cluster-backup.sh.
                                  # This pod runs exclusively on master nodes — the only
                                  # context where privileged access to etcd is acceptable.
                                  # ACS policy exception scoped to openshift-etcd namespace
                                  # on master nodes only.
              command: [/bin/bash, -ec, |
                  TIMESTAMP=$(date -u +%Y%m%d-%H%M%S)
                  BACKUP_DIR="/host/home/core/assets/backup"
                  BACKUP_NAME="etcd-backup-${TIMESTAMP}"

                  # Step 1: run cluster-backup.sh (OCP built-in backup tool)
                  chroot /host /usr/local/bin/cluster-backup.sh ${BACKUP_DIR}/${BACKUP_NAME}
                  # cluster-backup.sh produces:
                    #   etcd-snapshot-${TIMESTAMP}.db      — the etcd data snapshot
                    #   static_kubeconfig                  — cluster kubeconfig for restore
                    #   static-pod-resources.tar.gz        — OCP static pod manifests

                  # Step 2: upload all three files to ODF MCG bucket
                  for f in "${BACKUP_DIR}/${BACKUP_NAME}/"*; do
                    CHECKSUM=$(sha256sum "${f}" | cut -d' ' -f1)
                    aws s3 cp "${f}" \
                      "s3://${BUCKET_NAME}/${BACKUP_NAME}/$(basename ${f})" \
                      --endpoint-url "${BUCKET_ENDPOINT}" \
                      --metadata "sha256=${CHECKSUM}"
                    echo "Uploaded $(basename ${f}): SHA256=${CHECKSUM}"
                  done

                  # Step 3: verify upload by reading back and comparing checksums
                  for f in "${BACKUP_DIR}/${BACKUP_NAME}/"*; do
                    LOCAL_SUM=$(sha256sum "${f}" | cut -d' ' -f1)
                    REMOTE_META=$(aws s3api head-object \
                      --bucket "${BUCKET_NAME}" \
                      --key "${BACKUP_NAME}/$(basename ${f})" \
                      --endpoint-url "${BUCKET_ENDPOINT}" \
                      --query "Metadata.sha256" \
                      --output text)
                    [ "${LOCAL_SUM}" = "${REMOTE_META}" ] || \
                      { echo "CHECKSUM MISMATCH: $(basename ${f})"; exit 1; }
                  done

                  # Step 4: clean up local backup files (off-node copy is now authoritative)
                  rm -rf "${BACKUP_DIR}/${BACKUP_NAME}"

                  echo "etcd backup ${BACKUP_NAME} completed and verified."
              ]
              env:
                - name: BUCKET_NAME
                  valueFrom:
                    configMapKeyRef:
                      name: ncb-etcd-backups
                      key: BUCKET_NAME
                - name: BUCKET_ENDPOINT
                  valueFrom:
                    configMapKeyRef:
                      name: ncb-etcd-backups
                      key: BUCKET_HOST
                - name: AWS_ACCESS_KEY_ID
                  valueFrom:
                    secretKeyRef:
                      name: ncb-etcd-backups
                      key: AWS_ACCESS_KEY_ID
                - name: AWS_SECRET_ACCESS_KEY
                  valueFrom:
                    secretKeyRef:
                      name: ncb-etcd-backups
                      key: AWS_SECRET_ACCESS_KEY
              volumeMounts:
                - name: host-root
                  mountPath: /host
          volumes:
            - name: host-root
              hostPath:
                path: /
                                # Mount host root: cluster-backup.sh must run in the master node's
                                  # chroot environment to access the etcd static pod socket.
STORAGE-04 · Section 06
etcd restore runbook — the complete enterprise recovery procedure
This is the runbook for restoring the NovaCrestBank OCP cluster from an etcd snapshot. It is written to be executed by a trained operator in a real incident — not just a reference document. Every step includes the expected output and the action if that output is not seen.
When to execute this runbook vs when to use OADP restore
etcd restore is for cluster-level catastrophic failures: all three master nodes are lost, etcd data is corrupted cluster-wide, or the cluster cannot start at all (no API server available). It restores the entire OCP cluster to the state of the snapshot — all Namespace objects, all Deployments, all ConfigMaps, all RBAC. Application PVC data is NOT in the etcd snapshot — PVCs are restored separately via OADP (Section 13-14). etcd restore + OADP application restore is the complete cluster recovery procedure. OADP alone (without etcd restore) is for application-level recoveries where the cluster is still running: accidental namespace deletion, database corruption, application misconfiguration.
Step 1
Download the backup from ODF MCG
From the jumphost (or any machine with network access to the ODF MCG S3 endpoint), download the most recent backup set. Identify the backup timestamp: aws s3 ls s3://ncb-etcd-backups/ --endpoint-url http://s3.openshift-storage.svc:80 | sort | tail -3. Download all three files for the chosen backup: aws s3 sync s3://ncb-etcd-backups/etcd-backup-{TIMESTAMP}/ /tmp/etcd-restore/ --endpoint-url .... Verify checksums match the stored metadata. Expected output: three files in /tmp/etcd-restore/: the .db snapshot, static_kubeconfig, and static-pod-resources.tar.gz.
Step 2
Provision replacement master nodes (if needed)
If master nodes were destroyed in the disaster, provision three new master nodes using the same MachineConfig and MachineSet configuration. The nodes must have the same hostnames and IP addresses as the original masters (10.10.0.11, 10.10.0.12, 10.10.0.13) — etcd membership uses these addresses. If IP addresses cannot be reused, additional steps are required to update the etcd peer URLs. Expected: three RHCOS nodes booted and reachable via SSH from the jumphost.
Step 3
Copy backup files to the first master node
scp /tmp/etcd-restore/* core@10.10.0.11:/home/core/assets/backup/restore/. Verify files arrive with correct checksums. This master node will be the first to restore etcd — the other two masters will be recovered from it. Expected: all three files present on master-01 at /home/core/assets/backup/restore/.
Step 4
Run the OCP restore script on the first master
SSH to master-01. Run: sudo /usr/local/bin/cluster-restore.sh /home/core/assets/backup/restore/. This script: stops the static etcd pod, restores the etcd data from the snapshot, reconfigures etcd to run as a single-member cluster (master-01 only), and restarts the static pod. Expected output: Restoring etcd member... then Waiting for etcd to start...etcd member is restored. Time: 5–10 minutes. If this fails, check that all three backup files are present and that the snapshot file is not corrupted (verify SHA256).
Step 5
Verify API server recovery and cluster state
From master-01: export KUBECONFIG=/etc/kubernetes/static-pod-resources/kube-apiserver-certs/secrets/node-kubeconfigs/localhost.kubeconfig. Then: oc get nodes. Expected: all nodes listed (though some may be NotReady). oc get namespaces | wc -l should return the expected count. The Kubernetes API is serving again. Time from start of Step 4 to API available: 10–20 minutes.
Step 6
Recover the second and third etcd members
Force the remaining master nodes to rejoin the etcd cluster by deleting their existing etcd member pods: oc delete pod -n openshift-etcd etcd-master-02.ocp.corp.novacrestbank.com and etcd-master-03.... The etcd Operator detects the missing members and adds them back as new members, syncing data from master-01's restored state. Monitor: oc get pods -n openshift-etcd -w until all three etcd pods are Running. Expected: 3 etcd members, quorum restored. Time: 15–30 minutes.
Step 7
Recover all OCP Operators and then restore application data
After etcd is healthy, OCP operators begin reconciling — they compare the restored etcd state (namespace contents, CRDs, deployments) with the actual cluster state and recreate any missing objects. Wait for all cluster operators to reach Available=True: oc get co | grep -v 'True.*False.*False'. Expected: all operators Available. Time: 20–45 minutes. Then proceed to OADP application restore (Section 13-14) to restore application PVC data. Total estimated recovery time (Steps 1–7 + OADP restore): 2–4 hours for the Tier 2 systems.
STORAGE-04 · Section 07
What OADP is — Velero inside OpenShift, with Kubernetes-aware backup
OADP (OpenShift API for Data Protection) is Red Hat's supported distribution of Velero — the open-source Kubernetes backup tool — packaged as an OCP Operator with additional OCP-specific features. Where etcd backup captures the cluster state (Kubernetes objects), OADP captures both the cluster state and the application data (PVC contents via VolumeSnapshots) in a single, coordinated backup operation.

What OADP backs up

  • Kubernetes objects: Deployments, StatefulSets, ConfigMaps, Secrets, Services, Ingresses — everything in the selected namespaces
  • PVC contents: via Kubernetes VolumeSnapshots (ODF RBD and CephFS only — not vSAN PVCs)
  • CustomResources: ExternalSecrets, ApplicationSets, Routes, any CRD objects
  • RBAC: ServiceAccounts, Roles, RoleBindings within the namespace

What OADP does NOT back up

  • etcd cluster state — use the etcd CronJob (Section 05)
  • PVCs on vSAN StorageClasses (no VolumeSnapshot support — see STORAGE-03 Section 14)
  • MinIO bucket contents — handled by MinIO's own lifecycle rules and GitLab backup CronJob (GITOPS-00 Section 18)
  • CyberArk Conjur secret values — these are the authoritative source; backups reference paths, not values

STORAGE-04 · Section 09
DataProtectionApplication CR — every field explained
The DataProtectionApplication (DPA) is the OADP CR that configures the entire backup infrastructure: the backup storage location (ODF MCG), the volume snapshot provider (Ceph CSI), and the Velero deployment settings. One DPA configures everything OADP needs to operate.
DataProtectionApplication — NovaCrestBank OADP configuration
apiVersion: oadp.openshift.io/v1alpha1
kind: DataProtectionApplication
metadata:
  name: ncb-dpa
  namespace: openshift-adp
spec:
  configuration:
    velero:
      defaultPlugins:
        - openshift
                # openshift: required for OCP-specific resources (Routes, BuildConfigs,
                    # ImageStreams). Also handles SCC (SecurityContextConstraints) backup
                    # and restore mapping between source and destination.
        - aws
                # aws: provides the S3-compatible backup storage plugin.
                    # Despite the name, it works with any S3-compatible endpoint —
                    # including ODF MCG's NooBaa S3 gateway.
        - csi
                # csi: enables VolumeSnapshot-based PVC backup.
                    # The CSI plugin coordinates with the ODF Ceph CSI driver to
                    # take Kubernetes VolumeSnapshots during backup.
      resourceTimeout: 10m
            # Maximum time to wait for a resource operation during backup/restore.
              # Increase this for large PVC VolumeSnapshot operations that take longer.
    nodeAgent:
      enable: true
      uploaderType: kopia
            # nodeAgent (formerly restic): enables file-level backup for PVCs that
              # don't support VolumeSnapshots (vSAN PVCs).
              # kopia: the preferred uploader — faster and more efficient than restic.
              # Used as fallback when VolumeSnapshot is not available for a PVC.

  backupLocations:
    - name: default
      velero:
        provider: aws
        default: true
        objectStorage:
          bucket: oadp-backup-a1b2c3d4-xxxx
                    # The MCG bucket name from the OBC ConfigMap (STORAGE-01 Section 16).
                      # Substitute the actual generated bucket name here.
          prefix: velero
                    # prefix: all OADP backup objects are stored under velero/ in the bucket.
                      # This leaves room for other uses of the same bucket (e.g., etcd backups
                      # stored directly without the prefix).
        config:
          s3Url: http://s3.openshift-storage.svc:80
                    # Internal MCG S3 endpoint — Velero runs inside the cluster.
                      # Using the internal service URL avoids external network round-trips.
          region: us-east-1
                    # MCG accepts any region string — us-east-1 is conventional.
          s3ForcePathStyle: "true"
                    # Path style required for MCG: http://endpoint/bucketname/key
                      # instead of http://bucketname.endpoint/key (virtual hosted style).
                      # MCG does not support virtual hosted-style S3 addressing.

STORAGE-04 · Section 10
Backup schedules — per-tier frequency and retention
OADP Schedules are recurring Backup objects. Each Schedule creates a new Backup on its cron schedule. The TTL (time to live) on each Backup determines how long the backup is retained before OADP automatically deletes it from the MCG bucket.
OADP Schedule objects — three tiers, three frequencies
# ─── TIER 1: Critical — banking namespaces — every hour ──────────────────────
apiVersion: velero.io/v1
kind: Schedule
metadata:
  name: ncb-tier1-banking-hourly
  namespace: openshift-adp
spec:
  schedule: "0 * * * *"
    # Every hour at minute 0. Satisfies Tier 1 RPO of 1 hour.
  template:
    includedNamespaces:
      - banking-production
    includedResources:
      - "*"
    storageLocation: default
    snapshotVolumes: true
        # snapshotVolumes: true — take VolumeSnapshots of all ODF RBD PVCs in the namespace.
          # banking-production has: banking-postgres-data (ODF RBD) and banking-redis-data (ODF RBD).
          # Both will have VolumeSnapshots taken as part of each hourly backup.
    ttl: 72h0m0s
        # TTL 72 hours: hourly backups retained for 3 days = 72 recovery points.
          # Longer retention would consume significant MCG storage for hourly PVC snapshots.
    hooks:
      resources:
        - name: postgres-backup-hook
          includedNamespaces: [banking-production]
          labelSelector:
            matchLabels:
              app: banking-postgres
          pre:
            - exec:
                container: postgres
                command:
                  - /bin/bash
                  - -c
                  - psql -U postgres -c "CHECKPOINT;"
                                # Pre-backup hook: execute CHECKPOINT before the VolumeSnapshot.
                                  # PostgreSQL CHECKPOINT flushes all dirty buffers to disk,
                                  # ensuring the snapshot captures a consistent on-disk state.
                                  # Without CHECKPOINT: the snapshot may capture a state with
                                  # dirty buffers in memory but not on disk — recovery requires
                                  # WAL replay, which is slower and may fail if WAL is truncated.
---
# ─── TIER 2: Important — platform namespaces — every 6 hours ─────────────────

      - openshift-logging
      - openshift-monitoring

        # TTL 336 hours = 14 days. 4 backups/day × 14 days = 56 recovery points.
---

STORAGE-04 · Section 11
GitLab application backup — Gitaly source code and PostgreSQL
GitLab requires special handling: its Gitaly PVC (source code) is on vsan-gold, which does not support Kubernetes VolumeSnapshots. The node agent (kopia) provides file-level backup for the Gitaly PVC. GitLab's PostgreSQL (also vsan-gold) uses a pre-backup hook to create a consistent pg_dump before the kopia backup runs. Once GitLab is migrated to ODF RBD (STORAGE-03 Section 18 migration candidates), this switches to VolumeSnapshot backup automatically.
vSAN PVC backup — kopia file-level backup has a slower RTO than VolumeSnapshot
Kopia file-level backup copies every file from the Gitaly PVC to the OADP MCG bucket. For a 200 GiB Gitaly PVC, this takes significantly longer than a Ceph VolumeSnapshot (which is near-instantaneous as a copy-on-write snapshot). Restore time is also longer — kopia restores by copying files back, whereas VolumeSnapshot restore creates a new PVC from the snapshot in seconds. This is the practical consequence of GitLab being on vSAN: its backup and restore times are longer. Migrating GitLab Gitaly to ocs-storagecluster-ceph-rbd (STORAGE-03 Section 18) reduces the GitLab OADP backup window from ~2 hours to ~5 minutes and the restore window proportionally.

STORAGE-04 · Section 13
Restore concepts — full namespace vs selective vs cross-namespace
OADP supports three restore patterns, each appropriate for a different failure scenario. Understanding which pattern to use prevents restoring more than necessary (risking overwriting healthy data) or less than necessary (leaving the application in an inconsistent state).
Restore typeScenarioWhat is restoredRisk
Full namespace restore Entire namespace accidentally deleted, or namespace corrupted. All application objects and PVC data need recovery. All Kubernetes objects from the backup + all PVC data from VolumeSnapshots. The namespace is recreated exactly as it was at backup time. Any changes made after the backup timestamp are lost. If the namespace was partially working, those partial changes are overwritten.
Selective resource restore A specific Deployment or ConfigMap was accidentally deleted or misconfigured. The PVCs are intact and healthy. Only the specified Kubernetes objects from the backup. PVC data is NOT restored (snapshotVolumes: false in the Restore object) — the running PVCs are left intact. Lower risk than full restore. Targets only the missing or broken objects. PVC data remains current.
PVC data restore (database recovery) Database PVC is corrupted or has a logical error (bad migration, accidental data deletion). The application itself is running and healthy. Only the specified PVC, restored from a VolumeSnapshot. The application Deployment is scaled to 0, the PVC is restored, then the Deployment is scaled back up. Data between the snapshot time and the restore time is lost. The application experiences downtime during the restore. Test the restore in a non-production namespace first to verify the data is correct.
STORAGE-04 · Section 14
Application restore runbook — payment-service complete recovery
This runbook documents the complete procedure for restoring the banking-production namespace after a catastrophic failure, using the most recent Tier 1 hourly backup. It is written to be executed during a real incident — every command includes the expected output.
Step 1
Identify the most recent good backup
List the available Tier 1 backups and identify the most recent one with Phase=Completed: oc get backup -n openshift-adp -l velero.io/schedule-name=ncb-tier1-banking-hourly --sort-by=.metadata.creationTimestamp | tail -10. Choose the most recent backup before the incident. Note the backup name — e.g., ncb-tier1-banking-hourly-20260421110001. Verify the backup is complete: oc describe backup ncb-tier1-banking-hourly-20260421110001 -n openshift-adp | grep -A5 "Status:". Expected: Phase=Completed, Warnings=0 (or only known benign warnings).
Step 2
Delete the damaged namespace (if it exists)
If the banking-production namespace exists but is corrupted: oc delete namespace banking-production. Wait for the namespace to be fully terminated: oc get namespace banking-production should return "not found" before proceeding. If the namespace is stuck in Terminating, finalizers may need to be removed — check with oc get namespace banking-production -o jsonpath='{.spec.finalizers}' and remove them if needed.
Step 3
Create the Restore object
Apply the Restore CR targeting the chosen backup: oc apply -f restore-banking-production.yaml where the file contains the Restore object with backupName: ncb-tier1-banking-hourly-20260421110001, includedNamespaces: [banking-production], and restorePVs: true. OADP recreates the namespace, restores all Kubernetes objects, and creates new PVCs from the VolumeSnapshots embedded in the backup. Monitor: oc describe restore restore-banking-production -n openshift-adp.
Step 4
Wait for Restore Phase=Completed
oc get restore restore-banking-production -n openshift-adp -w. Expected phases: New → InProgress → Completed. Time: 5–15 minutes for VolumeSnapshot-based restore (fast — new PVC created from snapshot, then data is available immediately via Ceph COW). If Phase=PartiallyFailed: check the warnings/errors — often a single non-critical resource (like an HPA that references a missing metric server) fails without affecting the core application restore.
Step 5
Verify application health post-restore
Check all pods are Running: oc get pods -n banking-production. Check ExternalSecrets are synced (they will re-sync from CyberArk after restore): oc get externalsecret -n banking-production — all should show Ready=True within 5 minutes. Check the payment-service responds to health checks: curl -sk https://payment-service-production.apps.ocp.corp.novacrestbank.com/api/v1/payments/health. Expected: {"status":"UP"}. Document the recovery timestamp and the data loss window (time between backup timestamp and incident).
STORAGE-04 · Section 15
NovaCrestBank DR strategy — what the backup architecture covers and what it does not
The OADP + etcd backup architecture described in this guide provides a defined level of DR protection. Being explicit about what is and is not covered prevents false confidence and identifies gaps for future investment.

What this architecture covers

  • Accidental deletion: namespace or object accidentally deleted — restore from OADP within 15 minutes
  • Application data corruption: database corrupted by bad migration — restore PVC from VolumeSnapshot
  • OCP cluster failure: cluster cannot start — restore from etcd snapshot + OADP application data
  • Single storage node failure: Ceph 3× replication — no action needed, Ceph self-heals (HA, not DR)
  • Ransomware on application data: OADP snapshots in MCG bucket (separate access credentials from application) provide clean recovery point

What this architecture does NOT cover

  • Complete site loss: if the entire data centre (including the ODF storage nodes where MCG resides) is destroyed, the backups are also destroyed. A geographically separate backup target (AWS S3, secondary data centre) is required for true site-loss DR — a future ROSA hybrid cloud project.
  • Sub-1-hour RPO for Tier 1: hourly OADP snapshots achieve ~1-hour RPO. Transactions in the most recent hour may be lost. PostgreSQL WAL archiving to a separate target can reduce this to near-zero.
  • Hot standby (RTO < 15 minutes): OADP restore takes 5–15 minutes minimum. A hot standby cluster with synchronous replication could achieve sub-minute RTO — not implemented in the current architecture.

STORAGE-04 · Section 17
DR testing — the annual restore exercise for APRA CPS 234 compliance
APRA CPS 234 requires that DR plans are tested annually and results are documented. The DR exercise is not a theoretical walkthrough — it is an actual restore from backup in a controlled test environment, with measured timings and documented outcomes. This section defines the exercise scope, procedure, and success criteria.
Scope
What the annual exercise covers
The annual exercise must cover: (1) etcd restore — provision a test cluster or use a dedicated test environment, restore from an etcd snapshot, verify the cluster API is available; (2) OADP application restore for banking-production — using a backup that is at least 24 hours old (not the most recent — tests that aged backups are valid), restore the banking-production namespace including PVC data, start the banking applications, verify payment-service health endpoint responds; (3) Timing measurement — record actual RTO from "disaster declared" to "payment-service Healthy." The measured RTO must be within the declared 4-hour target for Tier 1 systems.
Evidence
Documentation for APRA attestation
The DR exercise produces a test report documenting: date and scope of the exercise, the backup timestamp used (to calculate the RPO demonstrated), the recovery start time and recovery completion time (to calculate the RTO demonstrated), any deviations from the runbook and corrective actions taken, names of personnel involved, and sign-off from the Head of Technology and Chief Risk Officer. This report is retained for the APRA prudential reporting cycle and is available for examination. Material gaps between declared targets and demonstrated actuals must be reported to the Board Risk Committee and remediated within a defined timeframe.
Cadence
Annual minimum — with scenario rotation
APRA requires annual testing as a minimum. NovaCrestBank's DR program rotates scenarios across years: Year 1 — application-level restore (OADP); Year 2 — cluster-level restore (etcd + OADP); Year 3 — combined scenario (etcd + application + etcd member re-addition). This rotation ensures all components of the DR architecture are tested over a 3-year cycle while meeting the annual testing requirement each year. Partial exercises (spot checks) of specific components can be run more frequently as part of platform maintenance operations.

Backup monitoring — alerts for missed and failed backups
A backup system without monitoring is not a backup system — it is a hope. Three PrometheusRules cover the critical backup failure modes: an OADP backup that fails, an OADP backup schedule that stops producing backups, and an etcd backup CronJob that fails or misses its window.
PrometheusRules — backup failure alerting
apiVersion: monitoring.coreos.com/v1
kind: PrometheusRule
metadata:
  name: ncb-backup-alerts
  namespace: openshift-adp
spec:
  groups:
    - name: ncb-backup
      rules:

        - alert: OADPBackupFailed
          expr: velero_backup_failure_total > 0
          for: 5m
          labels: {severity: critical}
          annotations:
            summary: An OADP backup has failed — the RPO may be exceeded
            runbook: Check oc describe backup -n openshift-adp for failure reason

        - alert: OADPTier1BackupMissed
          expr: |
                time() - max(velero_backup_last_successful_timestamp{
                  schedule="ncb-tier1-banking-hourly"}) > 7200
          for: 10m
          labels: {severity: critical}
          annotations:
            summary: "Tier 1 banking backup not completed in 2 hours — RPO at risk"
                        # Fires if no successful Tier 1 backup in 2 hours (2× the 1-hour RPO).
                          # This gives the on-call team time to investigate before the RPO
                          # is definitively breached.

        - alert: EtcdBackupMissed
          expr: |
                time() - max(kube_cronjob_status_last_successful_time{
                  cronjob="etcd-backup"}) > 25200
                    # 25200 seconds = 7 hours. Fires if etcd backup hasn't run in 7 hours
                      # (the 6-hour schedule + 1 hour grace period).
          for: 15m
          labels: {severity: warning}
          annotations:
            summary: "etcd backup CronJob has not run successfully in 7 hours"

        - alert: EtcdBackupFailed
          expr: kube_job_failed{job_name=~"etcd-backup-.*"} > 0
          for: 5m
          labels: {severity: critical}
          annotations:
            summary: "etcd backup job failed — investigate immediately"
